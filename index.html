The Boy Who Watched a Revolution

His father, Badar Hussain, was a PIA man who had spent thirty years travelling with presidents and prime ministers across fifty countries. In 1982, PIA posted him to the Philippines. He ate adobo, learned Tagalog phrases his schoolmates taught him, and grew up in a country that felt simultaneously warm and tense — like a pot of water that was almost, but not quite, boiling.

Then in February 1986, the water boiled. Two million Filipinos flooded EDSA highway. Marcos fled by helicopter, reportedly carrying several suitcases — a detail the boy remembered, because years later he would open a very different briefcase belonging to a very different kind of man. Cory Aquino stepped into the presidency of a nation that had just reinvented itself in four days.

Most ten-year-olds forget what they had for breakfast. This one remembered what a nation looks like when it decides to evolve. And every time he reads about a regime under pressure — whether it's the Iranian government, the Fed's credibility, or a market structure about to break — he doesn't panic. He recognises the pattern. Karachi in the 1990s had a peculiar talent: it could be terrifying and electrifying in the same afternoon. Curfews at 6 PM. Rehearsals at 8 PM. You learned to time your creative ambitions around the schedule of people trying to shut the city down. The city never cooperated with anyone's shutdown plans.

Khurram Badar wrote for Dawn in 1995 — Pakistan's most respected English daily — alongside Nadeem Farooq Paracha and Peerzada Salman. He did theatre at the Karachi Drama Circle, because apparently dodging street violence on the way to rehearsal builds character. He became GM Marketing at TCS, where a man named Khalid Nawaz Awan was quietly building Pakistan's logistics backbone from scratch. Twenty-eight years later, Khurram would return to TCS to build their AI platform. The circle closes slowly in Pakistan. But it closes.

His most special mentor during this time was Nadeem A Khan — a friendship built on shared language, shared ideas, and the kind of trust that only Karachi in the 90s could forge, when you literally trusted people with your safety on the way to a meeting.

The 1990s Karachi rule, which nobody writes down but everyone who was there knows: if you could build something in that city, during those years, under those conditions — you could build anything, anywhere, for the rest of your life.

He tested that rule across four continents. It held.

The Markets · 2024–Present

When Seven Trillion Dollars Vanished and One Man Said "4D Chess"

January 30, 2026. A perfectly ordinary Thursday. Donald Trump nominated Kevin Warsh as Fed Chair. Warsh: the man who resigned from the Fed in 2011 specifically because he disagreed with QE. A known hawk. A gold killer.

Gold crashed 15% in a single day. Silver crashed 31%. Seven trillion dollars destroyed in 48 hours. CME raised margins on gold from 5% to 9%. Margin calls cascaded. Every algorithm on the planet hit the sell button. Bloomberg's screens turned the shade of red usually reserved for cardiovascular emergencies.

Every analyst reversed their call. Every newsletter published an "urgent update" that said, essentially, "we were wrong, sell everything." It was the financial equivalent of a building evacuation, except nobody knew which building or which exit.

Khurram Badar looked at the same data everyone else was looking at. And he said three words that nobody on Wall Street said: "This is 4D Chess."

The thesis: Trump announced Warsh to crash gold — to punish the speculators, to prove he could move markets on a Thursday afternoon, to show power. But he kept Senator Tillis's blockade alive to prevent Warsh from actually being confirmed. Powell stays. QE at $40 billion a month continues. The crash was the point. The nomination was theatre. The confirmation was never the plan.

Two months later: Warsh is still blocked. Tillis is still holding. Powell is still doing QE. Gold recovered from $4,248 to $4,677. Silver recovered. The algorithms bought back everything they panic-sold.

The 4D Chess thesis — born from a man who has watched powerful men bluff from Manila to Musharraf to Trump — is intact. He didn't learn this from a Bloomberg terminal. He learned it from watching Marcos flee with suitcases. The powerful always tell you what they're doing. You just have to stop panicking long enough to notice.

What Was Inside Edhi Sahab's Briefcase

Of all the people Khurram Badar has driven around the UAE — and the list includes a Jackson — the one he talks about most wore the same charcoal shalwar kameez every single day for decades.

Abdul Sattar Edhi. Pakistan's greatest human being. The man who built the world's largest private ambulance network, who ran free morgues, orphanages, and hospitals, who was nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize, and who slept on a cot in a small room owning almost nothing. Edhi Sahab visited Dubai only twice, perhaps. Each time, he called Khurram. And Khurram came.

They drove to Ajman — to manage the Foundation's investments. Then to Habib Bank on Bank Street in Dubai, where Edhi conducted his business with the same matter-of-fact simplicity he brought to everything. The man ran a humanitarian empire spanning Pakistan, and he was handling it from the passenger seat of a car in the UAE with the administrative energy of someone paying a phone bill.

At some point during those hours, Khurram asked the question. The one everyone in Pakistan wanted to ask. He asked Edhi Sahab to open the famous briefcase — the one he carried everywhere, the one that had become mythological.

Edhi Sahab opened it.

Inside: a few documents. The famous charcoal shalwar kameez. A few basic items. That was all. The richest-hearted man in Pakistan carried less in his briefcase than most people carry in their pockets.

Here is what that briefcase taught a young entrepreneur from Karachi who would go on to build twenty platforms across four continents: accumulation is not the point. Impact is the point. Everything else is a briefcase.

Khurram Badar was on Edhi Sahab's personal call list. He does not say this to impress. He says it because it is the only credential that actually matters.

The Kingdom

Saudi Arabia in 1998 was a country between eras. Oil was at $12 a barrel — the lowest in a generation. The Kingdom was still decades away from Vision 2030, from MBS, from NEOM and the PIF's $700 billion ambitions. It was a quieter, more insular Saudi Arabia.

Khurram arrived and encountered something that would stay with him permanently: the scale of Gulf ambition, even when the money wasn't flowing. The infrastructure was already massive. Bahrain · Late 1999

The Island of Bankers

Bahrain at the turn of the millennium was the financial hub of the Gulf — before Dubai claimed that title. The tiny island kingdom had more banks per square kilometre than anywhere in the Middle East. For a young professional moving from Saudi Arabia to Bahrain, it was like moving from the factory floor to the control room. Saudi Arabia had the oil. Bahrain had the bankers who decided where the oil money went.

This was the formative exposure to Gulf banking that would later inform everything — from understanding ADIA and the Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth machine, to analysing the petrodollar system's vulnerabilities, to recognising that when Bahrain leads a UNSC Chapter VII resolution on Hormuz, it is not a small country punching above its weight.
